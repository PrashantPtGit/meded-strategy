Operating principle 1 — One story per phase

Paytm confuses people because it shouts forty things at once. We launch with one sentence ("report photo bhejo, apni bhasha mein samajh lo") and the app shows one big button. When users master that, we hand them the next thing. Every phase of the roadmap = one new sentence, never a pile of features.

Operating principle 2 — Downloads are vanity, uploads are truth

A download that never uploads a report is a poster that fell off the wall. Our real scoreboard is activation: % of installs that upload their first report. Target ≥ 40%. Every marketer's job is not "get installs" — it's "get a family's first report explained in front of them."

Operating principle 3 — Every install has a code

Every poster, reel link, and bio link carries its own referral/QR code (ANU, RIA-BPL-03, AMN-CTC-01…). We know exactly which lab counter in Bhopal and which reel produced each install. No code = it didn't happen. This is how a 4-person team learns faster than a 400-person team.

Operating principle 4 — The three never-do rules (unchanged from Briefing 02)

  • Never let AI diagnose. AI explains; only registered doctors advise. This is our legal shield and our trust story.
  • Never scale before one loop proves. MP + Odisha + Instagram is already three fronts. No new state until Day-90 numbers hold.
  • Never fight the government. Aarogya Setu 2.0 is infrastructure; we ride it (ABHA pull, Phase 2) and serve private healthcare — the lane it left open.

The CEO decision — phased, not everything together

We launch Phase 1 only. Here's why, and here's the order.

The question was: launch lean, or launch everything together? The answer is lean — and it isn't even close. Four reasons below, then the exact wave plan. The 42-feature map from Briefing 02 is still the destination; this page is just the driving order.

Why lean wins — four reasons

  1. The 6-week clock. Eight features ship in 6 weeks. Forty-two features ship never. We chose a date, so the date chooses the scope.
  2. Marketing needs ONE sentence. Ananya's reel, Ria's poster, and Aman's counter pitch all say the same thing: "Report photo bhejo, apni bhasha mein samajh lo." A 42-feature launch has no sentence — and a product without a sentence has no word of mouth.
  3. The anti-Paytm rule. A first-time user who opens the app and sees one big button UNDERSTANDS. A user who sees a grid of forty icons closes the app. Comprehension is a feature; clutter is a bug.
  4. Launch is our most fragile moment. Every extra feature is extra bugs, and Play Store reviews from week one follow us forever. Eight polished features earn 4.5 stars; forty rushed ones earn 3.2 — and 3.2 kills every poster Ria hangs.

Architecture — where every feature lives

Five tabs, one big button, zero confusion

The design test is one person: a 58-year-old on her first smartphone must upload a report and hear the explanation without anyone helping her. We call it the grandma test. Every screen below is built to pass it — and to stay the opposite of a Paytm home screen.

The seven design laws

  • 1 · Five tabs, never more. Ghar · Locker · SCAN (big centre button) · Family · Profile. New features live INSIDE these five — they never add a sixth.
  • 2 · Two taps to the magic. Open app → tap the big button → camera opens. That's it.
  • 3 · Language first. The very first screen asks हिंदी or English — before login, before anything.
  • 4 · Voice on everything. Every explanation screen has a big "Suno 🔊" button.
  • 5 · Grandma-grade sizes. 48dp minimum tap targets, 16pt minimum text, colour is never the only signal (from our Warm Clinic design system, already built in Week 1).
  • 6 · Buttons speak the user's action, in the user's language. "Report Samjhein", "Suno", "Doctor se Confirm Karein ₹49" — never "Submit", never "OCR", never jargon.
  • 7 · One job per screen. If a screen does two things, it becomes two screens.

Scope shield — how the date survives

  • Android first. India is overwhelmingly Android; iOS ships in month 2–3 from the same Flutter codebase. One store review process at launch, not two.

How we know it's working — in plain words

The scoreboard: six numbers, one sheet, every Monday

North star = reports explained per week. Not downloads, not followers — explained reports. Everything below exists to move that one number.

If a number misses — the response playbook (fix the machine, not the mood)

  • Installs low at a specific code → the poster location is wrong, not the product. Move it. Top-5 codes get doubled down; bottom-5 get relocated every two weeks.
  • Installs fine, activation low → onboarding problem, NOT a marketing problem. Prashant watches 5 real users open the app; fix where they hesitate. More marketing on a leaky bucket is money in the drain.
  • Activation fine, ₹49 low → the offer moment or the price story is off. Test copy first ("do chai se sasta"), placement second. Never discount below ₹49 — the price IS the positioning.
  • Share rate low → make sharing more visible on the result screen + Ananya runs a "bhejo jise zaroorat hai" push. Sharing is a button problem before it's a culture problem.
  • Retention low → reminders aren't landing. Check notification permissions flow — the fix is usually one screen.

Day-90 — the definition of success (the go/no-go review)

We gather (on a call) and check five boxes: 10,000 installs · ≥ 4,000 activated · ≈ 400 paid ₹49 events · ≥ 40 active QR points per state · 6+ panel doctors actually earning. Five ticks = Wave 3 (marketplace + first hospital pilot) gets the green light and we plan the next state. Three or four ticks = we fix before we grow. This discipline — prove the loop, then scale — is the whole lesson of the ₹35,000-crore health-app graveyard.
